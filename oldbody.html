Wells Fargo Offering Free FICO Scores to More Borrowers
Wells Fargo announced on Aug. 8 that it is offering mortgage borrowers and customers with home equity lines of credit, private student loans, personal loans, and certain auto loans free access to their FICO scores. The bank started offering free access to FICO scores to credit card customers in March. The scores are being provided through a partnership with Fair Isaac Corp., the creator of the FICO score, and more than 45 lenders already participate in the program. Discover Financial Services took the effort further by offering FICO scores to all consumers, not just its own customers, in recent months.

TIAA Agrees to Buy EverBank Financial for About $2.5 Billion
The financial services firm TIAA has agreed to acquire EverBank Financial Corp., Florida's biggest lender by deposits, in a $2.5 billion cash deal expected to close in the first half of 2017. EverBank is primarily an online bank with about 3,000 employees and 12 branches. The deal will add to TIAA's wealth management business and give it a platform for providing personal and business loans. Roger Ferguson, the former Federal Reserve vice chairman who leads TIAA, said, "EverBank's complementary capabilities and two decades of profitability make this an excellent investment and a great strategic fit for TIAA." EverBank has $27.4 billion in assets and $18.8 billion in deposits. "We are also pleased to be joining a company with a long-term focus, a deep commitment to the communities in which it operates and a desire to grow our franchise," said EverBank CEO Rob Clements.

It Won't Be Easy, But Small Banks Finally Have Some Hope
A recent survey by KPMG reveals that executives at banks with $1 billion to $20 billion in assets are confident they can increase revenue this year despite such challenges as low interest rates and regulation. However, doing so will not be easy as more bankers expect smaller institutions to struggle to survive. "Most of our clients will focus more on the quality of growth, and what it does for their earnings, rather than just growth for the sake of growth," said John Depman, national leader for regional and community banking at KPMG. According to the survey, about 40 percent of executives anticipate revenue growth of up to 5 percent this year, while 29 percent expect 6 percent to 10 percent growth. In addition, 52 percent of respondents are focused on cutting costs, and 59 percent are seeking organic growth, particularly in areas like wealth management and credit cards. About half of those polled believe banks need $1 billion in assets to stay independent, and 41 percent think $5 billion in assets is necessary. Furthermore, close to 45 percent of respondents said they are "likely" or "somewhat likely" to sell their institutions in the next year, up from 21 percent last year.

Former Piedmont Bancshares CEO Lands New Gig as SouthCrest Financial CEO
The $550 million-asset SouthCrest Financial Group Inc. in Woodstock, Ga., has appointed former Piedmont Bancshares President and CEO Brian D. Schmitt as president and CEO of SouthCrest Financial Group and its SouthCrest Bank subsidiary, effective Aug. 8. Schmitt — a community banker in Atlanta for three decades — also will become a director of both the bank and holding company. Christopher Marinac, managing principal and director of research for FIG Partners LLC, said, "In Atlanta, we have such a deep market of small and mid-sized companies that local banks are not covering all the available opportunities. It takes real leadership and focus, and Brian joins a short list of banks with this capacity." SouthCrest Bank has 12 branches throughout Georgia and Alabama.

Why You'll Always Need Cash, Despite PayPal, Apple Pay, Etc.
Mobile payments are making cash sparse in Sweden, but experts say a similar outcome is not expected anytime soon in the United States. Although studies show that more people are trying mobile payments, cash remains widely popular. A PYMNTS and InfoScout study found that 24 percent of respondents used Apple Pay in June, up from 13 percent a year ago. However, over the same period, the number of respondents who rarely consider using Apple Pay rose from 23 percent to 34 percent. Meanwhile, a 2014 Federal Reserve study revealed that cash is used in 40 percent of transactions, most under $20, and 30 percent of consumers said cash was their preferred way of paying. In addition, a survey by Intuit indicates that 55 percent of U.S. small businesses do not accept plastic. National Retail Federation general counsel Mallory Duncan says credit cards cost businesses up to 4 percent per transaction, and "if (mobile payments are) done in a way that simply replicates what credit cards do, they're going to suffer from the same sort of disfavoring from merchants as credit cards."

Call Reports for Small Banks Would Be Shorter Under New Plan
A proposal issued Aug. 5 by the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, the Federal Reserve, and the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. would permit banks with less than $1 billion in assets to file a short-form call report. "The agencies aimed to balance institutions' requests for a less burdensome regulatory reporting process with [their] need for sufficient data [to monitor banks]," said the Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council. "The agencies recognize that institutions operate under widely varying business models, which affects the nature and extent of their activities and translates into differences in the amount of information to be reported." The proposal — which the regulators plan to begin implementing on March 31, 2017 — also calls for some simplifying changes to larger banks' call reports.

Regions Reaches Agreement With DOJ in Mortgage Investigation
Birmingham, Ala.-based Regions Financial Corp. has reached a preliminary agreement with the U.S. Department of Justice to resolve an investigation into alleged mortgage abuses. In its quarterly regulatory filing submitted on Aug. 5, Regions said, "The settlement, which is subject to final review and approval by the Department of Justice, will not have a material effect on Regions' consolidated financial position." The bank did not disclose the settlement amount but indicated that it had previously set aside money to cover the costs. In 2013, Regions reported that it had received a subpoena from the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development tied to the origination of mortgages backed by the Federal Housing Administration.

A Payday-Loan Rival Gains Ground
The federal government's effort to rein in payday loans has given a boost to installment loans. Installment loans often carry triple-digit annual percentage rates like payday loans, but they tend to have longer repayment periods of six months to more than a year; may be for a few thousand dollars, compared to a few hundred dollars for payday loans; and are paid a little bit at a time, rather than in a single balloon payment like payday loans. Almost $24.2 billion in installment loans were given to borrowers with credit scores of 660 or less in 2015, up 78 percent from 2014 and nearly triple the amount in 2012, according to data from Experian. The increase in installment lending also has raised concerns at the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), which launched an inquiry into certain high-cost installment loans that fall outside its proposed rules for payday lending and many installment loans. Meanwhile, a recent report from the National Consumer Law Center indicates that lenders have structured installment loans in a way that can make them profitable when borrowers default and that installment loans can present bigger risks than payday loans because they keep borrowers indebted for a longer period.
